Color is more than just aesthetics; it's a powerful communication tool that influences user behavior, perception, and decision-making. In UI design, understanding color theory is essential for creating interfaces that are not only beautiful but also functional and user-friendly.

## The 60-30-10 Rule

One of the most popular frameworks for balancing color in UI design is the **60-30-10 rule**. It suggests that you should use:

*   **60% Primary Color:** This is your detailed neutral color (usually white, gray, or a very light tint). It serves as the background and creates space.
*   **30% Secondary Color:** This is your brand color. It supports the primary color and is used for cards, headers, or large graphical elements.
*   **10% Accent Color:** This is used for calls-to-action (CTAs), links, and highlighted states. It should contrast sharply with the other colors to draw attention.

> **Pro Tip:** When in doubt, start with a grayscale design. Add color only when you need to guide the user's attention.

## Color Psychology in UX

Different colors evoke different emotions and associations. While these can vary by culture, here are some general guidelines for digital products:

*   **Blue:** Trust, security, stability. (Used by banks, social networks)
*   **Red:** Urgency, excitement, error. (Used for clearance sales, delete buttons)
*   **Green:** Growth, success, money. (Used for finance apps, success states)
*   **Purple:** Creativity, luxury, wisdom. (Used by SaaS products, beauty brands)
*   **Orange:** Friendliness, energy, affordability. (Used for budget brands, call-to-actions)

## Accessibility First

Designing for accessibility isn't just a legal requirement; it's good business. Roughly 8% of men and 0.5% of women suffer from some form of color blindness.

1.  **Contrast Ratios:** Ensure your text has a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 against the background (WCAG AA standard).
2.  **Don't Rely on Color Alone:** Never use color as the only way to convey information. Use icons, text labels, or patterns to distinguish states (like error messages).

## Creating Harmony

Choosing a color palette from scratch can be daunting. Here are three foolproof methods to create harmonious schemes:

### 1. Monochromatic
Pick one base hue and create a palette using different tints (adding white), shades (adding black), and tones (adding gray). This creates a clean, sophisticated look that is easy to manage.

### 2. Analogous
Select three colors that are next to each other on the color wheel (e.g., Blue, Blue-Green, Green). This approach offers more variety while maintaining a calm, comfortable feel.

### 3. Complementary
Use two colors opposite each other on the color wheel (e.g., Blue and Orange). This creates high contrast and high impact, perfect for making CTAs pop.
